Ask a Question
Can my landlord evict me without a court order? +
No. In India, forcible eviction without a court order is illegal and can amount to a criminal offence under the Bharatiya Nyaya Sanhita (BNS). A landlord must obtain a decree from the Rent Court or Civil Court through the proper legal process. Even if you are behind on rent, you cannot be physically removed, have your locks changed, or your utilities disconnected without a court order. If your landlord attempts forcible eviction, you may approach the local Magistrate for an urgent order of restoration. The Model Tenancy Act, 2021 (adopted in several states) provides a structured Rent Court process for eviction disputes.
What are my rights if I am arrested? +
Upon arrest in India, you have the right to: be informed of the grounds of your arrest; consult a lawyer of your choice (and have a free one if you cannot afford it, through the DLSA); inform a relative or friend of your arrest; be produced before a Magistrate within 24 hours (excluding travel time); and remain silent — you cannot be compelled to be a witness against yourself (Article 20(3) of the Constitution). Under Section 41A BNSS, for offences punishable with less than 7 years, police must issue a notice before arresting — they can arrest without it only in specified situations. Do not resist arrest physically, but clearly assert your legal rights and ask for a lawyer immediately.
My employer fired me without any warning — is this legal? +
Generally, no — especially if you are a "workman" under the Industrial Disputes Act, 1947 / Industrial Relations Code. Dismissal for misconduct requires a fair domestic inquiry: you must be given a charge sheet, an opportunity to submit a reply, a fair hearing, and a speaking order. Dismissal without a proper inquiry process is considered procedurally unfair and can be challenged before the Labour Court. If your establishment has 100+ workers, retrenchment also requires government approval. You may file a dispute with the Labour Court or approach the Labour Commissioner. Do not delay — limitation periods apply. Free legal aid is available through the DLSA.
Can a debt collector threaten me or call me at any time? +
No. RBI guidelines strictly regulate how recovery agents may contact borrowers. They may not: visit you before 7am or after 7pm; use threatening, abusive, or obscene language; contact your employer, family, or neighbours to discuss your debt; or misrepresent themselves as court officials. They must carry an authorisation letter from the bank. If an agent harasses you, file a complaint with: (1) the bank's Grievance Officer; (2) the RBI Integrated Ombudsman at cms.rbi.org.in; (3) the police under BNS provisions for intimidation or criminal harassment; and (4) the National Helpline at 14448. Keep records of all calls, texts, and visits.
What happens if someone dies without a will? +
If a Hindu person dies without a will (intestate), the estate is distributed under the Hindu Succession Act, 1956. Class I heirs — the widow, sons, daughters, and mother — inherit equally. For a female Hindu, the property passes to her children and husband first. Importantly, since the 2005 amendment (upheld by the Supreme Court in 2020), daughters have equal rights in ancestral property from birth. For Muslims, succession follows Muslim Personal Law. Christians and Parsis follow the Indian Succession Act, 1925. An administrator must be appointed by the court through Letters of Administration, and the process can be slow and contentious — which is why having an updated, registered will is strongly recommended.
Can I cancel a contract after signing it? +
It depends on the type of contract and the circumstances. Under the Consumer Protection Act, 2019, you may cancel certain consumer agreements with reasonable notice, though a cancellation fee may apply. For agreements entered into due to direct marketing (a seller approaching you unsolicited), e-commerce rules and CPA provisions may allow a return or cancellation window. Contracts obtained through coercion, fraud, misrepresentation, undue influence, or mistake are voidable under the Indian Contract Act, 1872 — meaning you may rescind them. Contracts with illegal objects are void from the outset. If you want to cancel a contract, consult a lawyer and act quickly — delay can amount to affirmation of the contract.
How long does a debt last before it prescribes? +
Under the Limitation Act, 1963, most debts (including credit card dues, personal loans, and store accounts) become time-barred after 3 years from the date they became due or from the last acknowledgement or payment. Once time-barred, a creditor cannot file a recovery suit in court. However, the limitation period restarts if you make a payment (even partial) or give a written acknowledgement of the debt. Bank loans secured by property (mortgages) have a 12-year limitation. A court decree for a debt is enforceable for 12 years. Always seek legal advice before acknowledging or making any payment on an old debt — even a small payment can revive the limitation period.
